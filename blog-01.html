Why I Think Every Lawyer Should Specialise in Data Protection

A
Abdullah Ishaq Law student, OAU · Data Protection & AI Governance · April 2026

one sunny Tuesday afternoon, my commercial law class got cancelled. i had been planning to walk to the library, catch up on some reading, get ahead on a topic i had been putting off.

i did not make it to the library.

on the way there, my dad called. we exchanged greetings and then without any preamble he asked: what area of law do you want to specialise in?

i was in my third year. still trying to understand the difference between a condition and a warranty under the Sale of Goods Act. still confused about nemo dat and whether i would ever actually need to explain it to a real person. the idea of choosing a specialisation had not seriously crossed my mind.

i told him i would think about it. he gave me his usual talk — the kind that sounds like pressure but is really just a father trying to make sure his son is not floating. we said goodbye and i turned back toward my apartment instead of the library.

i sat down with the question.

what i actually did next

i did what any law student would do. opened my laptop and started searching. best areas of law to specialise in nigeria. emerging legal practice areas. highest paying legal specialisations in africa. the results came back predictable: corporate law, oil and gas, litigation, IP, project finance. same names i had been hearing since year one.

i read through maybe ten articles. most of them felt like they were written for someone who already knew they wanted to spend thirty years drafting shareholders agreements. that was not me. my vague plan — if you could call it a plan — was something tech-related. that was as far as i had gotten.

then i came across something that made me read a paragraph twice. one article mentioned that nigeria had passed a comprehensive data protection law in 2023. i had seen the headlines but never thought seriously about what it meant for legal practice. i clicked through. then i opened the actual text of the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023.

i did not look up for a long time.

so what is data protection actually about

here is the thing about data that i did not fully understand before i started reading this law.

your data is not just your name and phone number. your data is your location history at 2am, your spending patterns, your health conditions, your contact list, your browsing behaviour, what you search for when nobody is watching. it is the profile that gets built about you through hundreds of small digital interactions every day. and the people holding that profile have real power over you — whether you know it or not.

let me give you an example that is not hypothetical.

between 2019 and 2023, multiple nigerian loan apps were doing this: you open the app, you need money, you click through the terms without reading them properly because the text is tiny and you are in a hurry. the app gets access to your contacts, your location, your transaction history. somewhere down the line — through a sale, a breach, a third party the app was sharing data with — that information ends up somewhere you never intended.

now you are getting calls from lenders you never approached. debt collectors are calling your family members. people who know you are financially vulnerable are targeting you with products designed specifically for people who are financially vulnerable. your personal crisis became someone else's business model.

your personal crisis became someone else's business model. that is a data protection problem. and it took a data protection law to address it properly.

the Central Bank of Nigeria eventually moved on the harassment side of this. but the underlying problem — personal data being collected, shared and weaponised with no legal accountability — that is a data protection problem. and it took a data protection law to address it properly.

that law is the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023. it says personal data can only be processed with a lawful basis. it gives every Nigerian real rights over their information. it places binding obligations on every organisation that collects and uses personal data. it established the Nigeria Data Protection Commission as a dedicated regulator with the power to investigate, fine and sanction.

Your rights under the NDPA 2023
Know what personal data is held about you
Correct inaccurate data
Request deletion of your data
Restrict how your data is used
Receive your data in a portable format
Object to how your data is being processed

How Nigeria got here
2019
NDPR issued by NITDA. Nigeria's first data protection framework. Subsidiary legislation, limited enforcement.
2023
NDPA signed into law. Primary legislation. Independent regulator — the NDPC — established.
Mar 25
GAID issued. General Application and Implementation Directive. The new implementing framework.
Sep 25
GAID comes into force. NDPR 2019 ceases to apply. NDPC begins active enforcement.
Aug 25
1,368 organisations probed. Banking, insurance, pensions, gaming — sector-by-sector enforcement begins.
2026
Enforcement intensifies. Audit deadline 30 May. Penalties up to 2% gross revenue. More sectors incoming.

why this matters for every lawyer not just the tech ones

i want to address this directly because a lot of law students look at data protection and think it is someone else's field. the tech people, the legaltech crowd, the people who understand how apps work.

it is not.

think about what an employment lawyer does. employee records, performance data, health information, surveillance at work. all of that sits inside a data protection framework. think about family law — digital evidence, surveillance, the privacy of children online. think about criminal law and how evidence obtained through data collection gets challenged in court. think about corporate law and the contracts that have to be drafted between data controllers and the processors who handle their data. think about health law and the obligations around patient records as hospitals go digital.

data protection is not a niche inside technology law. it is the legal infrastructure underneath every area of practice that touches information about people. and every area of practice touches information about people.

the lawyers who understand that early are the ones who will be genuinely useful to organisations trying to figure this out. and most organisations are still trying to figure this out. the gap between what the NDPA requires and what nigerian businesses are actually doing is enormous.

that gap is not a problem for lawyers who know this field. it is a market.

where i am and where i am going

i am a third-year law student. i have not practised a day of law professionally. i want to be clear about that because i am not presenting expertise i do not have.

what i am is someone who has looked at where the legal profession is going and made a deliberate choice about where to build. i have read the NDPA 2023 and started working through the GAID. i have started studying the GDPR for comparative context. i am documenting my learning publicly and studying real enforcement decisions to understand what this law looks like in practice not just on paper.

i am building toward a career as a Data Protection Officer. this blog is part of that process — a learning journal that is public. every entry will have the main piece plus four recurring sections: what i studied that week, one enforcement case breakdown, resource recommendations and upcoming events in the privacy space.

if you are a law student figuring out your lane, i hope this helps. if you already work in this space, i would genuinely like to hear how you got there.

Learning Timeline

What i studied

Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 — full first read-through. Part I (preliminary), Part II (NDPC establishment) and Part III (data subject rights). Also started the GAID which replaced the NDPR in September 2025.

What excited me

The automated decision-making provision. Data subjects have the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal or significant effects. In a world of AI credit scoring and hiring algorithms, this is going to be one of the most contested provisions in years ahead.

What confused me

The interaction between consent and legitimate interest as lawful bases. Both are valid under the NDPA but the boundary between them in practice — especially for small Nigerian businesses — is not as clear as the statute makes it look.

What i am studying next

The GAID in full — it introduces new obligations including specific requirements for data protection impact assessments and compliance audit returns. Also starting GDPR Chapters I and II for comparative context.

Weekly Digest

NDPC · April 2026

NDPC extends 2025 audit return deadline to 30 May 2026. The commission gave organisations additional time to file compliance audit returns but signalled stricter enforcement going forward. The extension is not a reprieve — it is a window that closes.

NDPC Enforcement · August 2025

1,368 organisations probed across banking, insurance, pensions and gaming. All received 21-day ultimatums to demonstrate NDPA compliance. The NDPC described this as the beginning of sector-by-sector enforcement across the entire economy.

Regulatory Update · September 2025

GAID now in force. NDPR 2019 no longer applies. Any organisation still running a compliance programme built around the old NDPR needs to update it immediately against the GAID 2025.
